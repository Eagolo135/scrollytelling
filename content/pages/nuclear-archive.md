# Nuclear Engineering Through Time

Explore the evolution of nuclear science and engineering through carefully curated visual stories and historical technical achievements.

## The Atoms Era

The foundational period that unlocked atomic understanding, beginning with quantum mechanics breakthroughs and extending through the first controlled chain reaction experiments.

**Key Moments:**
- 1938: Nuclear fission discovery
- 1942: First controlled chain reaction (Chicago Pile-1)
- 1945: First nuclear power generation demonstration
- 1951: EBR-I achieves electricity production

## Reactor Technology Evolution

From experimental designs to modern safety-enhanced architectures, reactor design represents decades of engineering refinement and operational learning.

### Generation I – Early Commercial
- Magnox reactors (UK)
- CANDU (Canada) 
- Early PWR and BWR designs (US)

### Generation II – Standard Commercial
- Enhanced safety systems
- Standardized designs across regions
- Extended operating lifetimes
- 1960s-1980s deployment

### Generation III – Modern Advanced
- Passive safety systems
- Proliferation resistance
- Extended design life (60+ years)
- Operational efficiency gains

### Generation IV – Next-Generation Research
- Molten salt reactors
- Fast neutron reactors
- High-temperature gas reactors
- Advanced fuel cycles

## Materials and Physics

Nuclear engineering pushed materials science to new frontiers, requiring alloys and compounds to withstand extreme conditions:

- Neutron irradiation resistance
- Creep behavior under thermal stress
- Stress corrosion cracking prevention
- Long-term dimensional stability

## Safety Milestones

From catastrophe learning to defense-in-depth engineering, safety evolution defines modern nuclear operations:

- 1979: Three Mile Island lessons
- 1986: Chernobyl assessment and redesign
- 2011: Fukushima safety enhancements
- Modern probabilistic risk assessment

## Global Fleet Today

Operating nuclear power plants across 30+ nations:

- **Total Capacity:** 400+ GW
- **Annual Output:** ~2,700 TWh
- **Fleet Average Age:** 32 years
- **Safety Record:** Among lowest incident rates of any energy source

## Engineering Excellence Markers

Modern nuclear operations represent some of the most tightly controlled, monitored, and continuously improved industrial processes:

- Real-time reactor physics monitoring
- Predictive maintenance systems
- Advanced control room automation
- International cooperation and knowledge-sharing
